Maps are such an elegant solution to orienting yourself in space.

Earth's terrain folds into an array of diverse shapes and is covered with so many things, but a map cuts through all of this with stunning clarity.

Features get blocked together and notable landmarks bloom into an organic grid of direction and scale.

Maps increase the navigational signal to noise ratio to help lost people find their way.

The beauty of this is that they can be especially flexible to best serve the area that they represent, which sometimes lead to strange maps for strange places.

An early 20th century traveler braving the inhospitably hot Sahara desert might have seen such a map. Something uncommon is marked here.

While most maps depict whole regions filled with trees, here... there exists only one. L'Abre du Ténéré; the loneliest tree on the planet Earth.

Oh! Uh, it's marked L'Abre du Ténéré on most maps because that's what French colonizers called it.

I'll probably use these names interchangeably, except for the French name, because my mouth struggles to move like that.

Anyway...

By all appearances, this tree is ordinary. It's an Acacia tree, which is a common sight in Africa.

It bears a lean and stands a meager 10 feet tall. This species could grow up to 70 feet in favorable conditions.

The tree is cleaved in twain by a fork in the main branch, while thinning and wiry foliage provides the meagerest of shape. A trove of pale yellow flowers bloom in a mess of thorns.

It's incredible as any tree, but still ordinary.

It was once considered the loneliest tree in the world, or in other words, the most remote tree. It is the tree that is furthest from any other tree as best as can be known.

It's located on a continent known as Africa. Within that, a desert that goes by Sahara. And within that, deep inside of the country called Niger, there is a desert within the desert known as Ténéré.

It's easy to forget this about the Sahara, but it's only a desert half of the time. You see, the Sahara cycles between a hyper-arid desert climate and a savanna grassland climate. But this change happens over tens of thousands of years.

This tree might be a remnant from a Green Sahara, when there was once many Acacia. But as Earth's orientation sways, the Sahara bakes until the trees desiccate and relent to the dunes.

The Ténéré tree was only a memory even while it still lived. It was the only tree still standing, for hundreds of kilometers in any direction.

The key to its survival can be found underground, where a sprawling network of roots reach down 100 feet deep enough to reach the water table. This was discovered in the 1930s when the first of two wells were dug nearby. It was in a healthy state at this point, but that wouldn't last forever.
